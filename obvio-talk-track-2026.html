Part 1 — Q&A 15:00–30:00

Likely questions. Know these cold.

Why not Charlotte / Raleigh directly?

Large city deals run 12–18 months. Suburbs convert pilots in 90 days. That's the proof base that earns bigger systems. I'm sequencing, not avoiding.

How do you prioritize?

Four criteria: recent incident, active budget, identifiable committee, pre-seedable advocacy. Fail two of those → T3 nurture. I don't burn dials on cities that can't run a pilot.

What does 30-day success look like?

Eight qualified first meetings — they named a corridor and agreed to a follow-up with the AE. Metric is meeting quality, not dials sent.

How do you set Mike up for a good second meeting?

Context packet: buying committee map, flagged corridor, surfaced objections, matched case study. A second meeting that starts from context, not from scratch.

Why these 18 cities specifically?

Scored by buying trigger, not population. Huntersville: +34% growth in 5 years. Chapel Hill: published pedestrian task force. Every city has a specific reason on the list.

What if a city goes quiet?

One follow-up anchored to a new signal — council agenda, local news, NCDOT report. No response after three touches → T3. I don't chase silence.

Objection Map

Stay calm. Clarify. Reframe to a pilot.

"We already enforce this."

"That makes sense. The gap I usually hear isn't whether you enforce — it's whether you can cover the same area consistently enough to change behavior and bring back hard data. Is that a factor here?"

"This sounds politically sensitive."

"It is — which is exactly why the first step should be a narrow pilot with clear safety framing, not a broad rollout request. The data is what makes the council vote possible."

"No budget right now."

"Understood. I'm not calling to force a budget conversation. I want to understand if there's a corridor where a pilot gives you the data to build the case internally before the next cycle."

"Send me information first."

"Absolutely — I'll send the Morningside case study. What's the best email? And so I know what to highlight: is pedestrian safety or intersection enforcement the bigger priority right now?"

"We tried this before, it didn't work."

"What broke down — the technology, the community reaction, or procurement?" [Listen] "The council vote usually fails when there's no community voice upstream. That's the piece we help fix before the vote."

"Is this legal in NC?"

"Yes — I'll get you the NC legal brief. Is the legal question the main gate, or is budget the bigger one right now?" [Turn it into a discovery moment.]

Debrief Q&A 45:00–60:00

What they actually want to hear.

"What would you do differently?"

"I should have gotten them to name the intersection before I explained the product. If they name it, I've already won half the call." Name one thing. Then stop.

"How would you handle [unanticipated objection]?"

Acknowledge it. Classify it — political, operational, or budgetary. Reframe to a narrow pilot or data-gathering next step. Never argue. Always stay concrete.

"What's your definition of a good call?"

"They named a corridor. They told me who else needs to be in the room. And we have a specific next meeting on the calendar — not 'I'll think about it.'"

"What would 90 days look like?"

"Eight qualified meetings in cycle one. Three T1 pilots in scoping by day 60. One T2 city with advocacy pre-seeded. And a playbook Mike can run in market two without rebuilding from scratch."
