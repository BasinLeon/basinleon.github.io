Part 1 — Q&A
15:00 – 30:00 min

Likely questions. Know these cold.

Why not start with Raleigh or Charlotte directly?
Large city deals run 12–18 months. Suburbs and university towns convert pilots in 90 days. That's the proof base that gets you into bigger systems. I'm not avoiding them — I'm sequencing.
How do you prioritize when everything looks urgent?
Four criteria: recent incident, active budget, identifiable committee, pre-seedable advocacy. If a city fails two of those, it drops to T3 nurture. I don't burn dials on cities that can't operationalize a pilot.
What does success look like at 30 days?
Eight qualified first meetings — meaning the prospect named a specific corridor and agreed to a follow-up with the AE. That's the metric. Not dials, not emails sent.
How do you set Mike up for a good second meeting?
Every meeting gets a context packet: buying committee map, the corridor they flagged, objections I already surfaced, and a matched case study. A second meeting that starts from context, not from scratch.
Why these 18 cities and not others?
I scored by buying trigger, not population. Huntersville grew 34% in five years — that's an enforcement gap story. Chapel Hill has a published pedestrian safety task force — that's a warm political floor. Every city has a reason.
How do you handle a city that goes quiet?
One follow-up anchored to a new signal — a council agenda item, a local news story, a NCDOT report. If there's no signal and no response after three touches, it moves to T3. I don't chase silence.
Part 2 — Role Play
30:00 – 45:00 min · Live cold call or discovery

Open clean. Find the corridor. Close to a next step.

O
The opener — 30 seconds, earn 60 more
"[Name], Leon with Obvio AI. I'm mapping traffic enforcement gaps across the Charlotte metro — do you have 60 seconds?

The reason I'm calling is that cities like yours usually already know where the risky intersections are, but they don't have enough officer coverage or current data to move on them. Obvio helps cities create deterrence and real violation data without waiting on a long infrastructure cycle.

Is there a corridor or school-zone area that comes up repeatedly for your team?"
E
Explore — find the three things
The corridor. The enforcement gap. The internal blocker.
"Where do complaints or incidents cluster most often today?"

"What data do you already have on that area, and where is it thin?"

"If a pilot made sense — who else would need to be in that conversation internally?"
A
Advance — close to a specific next step, not a vague follow-up
"If you point me to that intersection, I can put together what a thirty-day pilot would look like — the data you'd get, the framing for your manager, and a case study from a city your size that's already done this. Can we get thirty minutes with you and the Chief next week?"
The Proving Ground Close — use when they're warm but hesitant

"Chief, even if you had thirty more officers, you couldn't enforce every stop sign. Chief Franklin in Morningside reduced violations by 50% in eight weeks. Let me put devices at your worst intersection and give you something real to take to your council."

Objection Map
Live role play + debrief

Stay calm. Clarify. Reframe to a pilot.

"We already enforce this."
"That makes sense. The gap I usually hear isn't whether you enforce — it's whether you can cover the same risky area consistently enough to change behavior and bring back hard data. Is that a factor here?"
"This sounds politically sensitive."
"It is, which is exactly why the first step should be a narrow pilot with clear safety framing and measurable results — not a broad rollout request. The data is what makes the council vote possible."
"No budget right now."
"Understood. I'm not calling to force a budget conversation. I want to understand whether there's a corridor where a pilot would give you the data to build the case internally before the next cycle."
"Send me information first."
"Absolutely — I'll send you the Morningside case study. What's the best email? And so I know what to highlight — is pedestrian safety or intersection enforcement the bigger priority for you right now?"
"We tried something like this before."
"That's useful to know. What broke down — was it the technology, the community reaction, or the procurement process?" [Listen, then:] "The council vote usually fails when there's no community voice upstream. That's the piece we help fix before the vote."
"Is this legal in North Carolina?"
"Great question — I'll get you our legal brief for NC specifically. The short answer is yes, and I can walk through the enabling statute. Is the legal question the main gate, or is budget the bigger one right now?"
Part 2 — Debrief Q&A
45:00 – 60:00 min

What they actually want to hear.

"What would you do differently?"
Name one specific question you should have asked earlier. "I should have gotten them to name the intersection before I explained the product. If they named it, I already won half the call." Then stop.
"How would you handle [objection you didn't anticipate]?"
Acknowledge it. Classify it — political, operational, or budgetary. Then reframe to a narrow pilot or data-gathering next step. Never argue. Always stay concrete.
"What's your definition of a good call?"
"They named a corridor. They told me who else needs to be in the room. And we have a specific follow-up on the calendar — not 'I'll think about it.'"
"What would you do with this territory in 90 days?"
"Eight qualified meetings in the first cycle. Three T1 pilots in scoping by day 60. One T2 city with advocacy pre-seeded before the council vote. And a playbook Mike can run in market two without me rebuilding it from scratch."
