Core thesis

Do not start by spraying every city in the state. Start where a pain signal is visible, the buying committee is navigable, and a pilot can become a public proof point inside 90 days.

Interview brief

Part 1 covers target selection, weekly structure, messaging, and qualification. Part 2 is a cold call or discovery role play.

Design choice

Clean, mission-first framing mirrors Obvio's site: clear problem, real community proof, minimal noise, operator language.

Speaker cue

Open with mission, then say: “I’m not pitching a list. I’m pitching a repeatable market-entry motion.”

Market logic

Why North Carolina, and why not start with Raleigh or Charlotte proper?

Large city deals can validate the category, but they are slower, more political, and harder for a first BDR motion to convert quickly. The right move is to win in smaller municipalities with visible traffic pain, then use those proofs to climb into larger metros.

Fast proof base
Suburbs and university towns can move from first meeting to pilot faster than top-tier metros.
Early wins create local proof for Chiefs, City Managers, and council members in adjacent markets.
Higher relevance
North Carolina offers multiple simultaneous buying triggers: rapid suburban growth, campus pedestrian density, and corridor safety pressure.
That means parallel campaigns with distinct messaging instead of one generic statewide pitch.
Answer for Q&A
“Large municipal deals run 12 to 18 months. Suburbs and university towns get you pilots and case studies in 90 days.”
“That proof base is what earns the right to go after bigger city systems next.”
Selection rule
Prioritize cities where I can identify a public safety pain point, a reachable buying committee, and a plausible pilot path before procurement complexity takes over.
The point is not to avoid the biggest cities forever. The point is to sequence the market so the first six months create evidence, references, and internal leverage.
Decision lens

Pain visibility + buying committee access + pilot probability + adjacency value.

What this signals

You understand territory design as pipeline architecture, not just account list generation.

Speaker cue

Say “I want proof density before prestige logos.” That is a sharp operator line if delivered calmly.

Three simultaneous motions

Segment the state by buying trigger, not geography alone.

Tier 1 · High-growth sprawl

Charlotte belt municipalities

Huntersville, Matthews, Concord, Monroe, Gastonia, Kannapolis.

Trigger: growth outpacing enforcement capacity and school-zone complexity.
Message: give Chiefs data and deterrence where they cannot station officers consistently.
Why first: high pilot probability and strong adjacency for regional expansion.
Tier 2 · Pedestrian density

Triangle and university towns

Chapel Hill, Carrboro, Cary, Durham, Greenville, Boone.

Trigger: student foot traffic, Vision Zero language, politically visible safety concerns.
Message: protect high-footfall corridors with evidence chiefs can take to managers and councils.
Why second: strong story fit, slightly more stakeholder complexity.
Tier 3 · Corridor and seasonal pressure

Coastal and I-40 corridor

Wilmington, Jacksonville, Fayetteville, Burlington, High Point, Wilson.

Trigger: seasonal surges, military or freight traffic, corridor speeding and crash concentration.
Message: frame Obvio as fast-response infrastructure when staffing and capital projects lag.
Why nurture: useful pipeline depth while Tier 1 and Tier 2 convert.
Operating principle

Each segment gets its own messaging lane, target titles, and proof framing. Same product, different trigger story.

Proof anchor

Messaging rules

What stays constant
Lead with the city's pain, not the product spec.
Reference proof points without overselling certainty.
Ask for a narrow next step: 15 minutes around one known corridor or intersection.
Write for speed and clarity. These are municipal operators, not marketing readers.
Why this works

The copy sells political cover and evidence generation, not just cameras. That is closer to how Chiefs and Managers think.

Messaging discipline

Each message changes the trigger story. The CTA stays small and concrete.

Speaker cue

Say “I would rather send fewer credible emails than blast one template across all 18 cities.”

Phone script

Cold call opener, discovery pivot, and objection handling.

Opener

30-second door opener
[Name], Leon with Obvio AI. I'm mapping traffic enforcement gaps across the Charlotte metro. Do you have 60 seconds? The reason I'm calling is that cities like yours usually already know where the risky intersections are, but they do not have enough officer coverage or current data to move quickly. Obvio helps cities create deterrence and evidence without waiting on a long infrastructure cycle. Is there a corridor or school-zone area that comes up repeatedly for your team?

Discovery pivot

From interruption to conversation
Where do complaints or incidents cluster most often today?
What data do you already have, and where is it thin or outdated?
Who else needs to be convinced internally if a pilot makes sense?
What has stopped this from becoming a higher-priority project already?

“We already enforce this.”

I would agree with the intent, then reframe on consistency: “That makes sense. The gap I usually hear is not whether you enforce, but whether you can cover the same risky area consistently enough to reduce behavior and bring back hard data.”

“This sounds politically sensitive.”

“It is sensitive, which is exactly why the first step should be a narrow pilot with clear safety framing and measurable results, not a broad rollout request.”

“No budget right now.”

“Understood. I am not calling to force a budget conversation on the first call. I am trying to understand whether there is a corridor where a pilot would help you build the case internally.”

Close

“If you point me to the one intersection or corridor you worry about most, I can make the next conversation concrete instead of generic.”

Why “mapping” works

It signals research and pattern recognition, not a random cold pitch.

Cold-call standard

Success is not closing the deal. Success is earning a specific next conversation around a known problem area.

Speaker cue

Read the opener slowly. It should sound observant, not hungry.

Qualification + handoff

What makes a lead qualified, and how I set my sales partner up for success.

Qualified meeting definition
The prospect identifies a specific problem corridor, school zone, or intersection.
There is a plausible internal path beyond curiosity: Chief, Manager, transportation, or council access.
They agree to a real follow-up meeting to review pilot logic or proof points.
Disqualify early if
The pain is vague and there is no urgency.
No one can name who else needs to be involved internally.
The conversation is purely informational with no willingness to review a concrete next step.
AE handoff packet
Account summary and segment hypothesis.
Named stakeholders and buying committee map.
Problem corridor or intersection details.
Objections already surfaced and language that resonated.
Recommended proof point to use in the next meeting.
My job in the handoff
Reduce ambiguity for Mike and Craig.
Make the second conversation sharper than the first.
Keep learning loops tight so messaging and targeting improve weekly.
A qualified meeting is my definition, not the prospect's. If they cannot point to a real problem area and a real next internal conversation, it is not yet qualified.
Why this matters

This is where BDR work stops being activity support and starts becoming sales infrastructure.

Debrief angle

If they ask what success looks like, say: “A second meeting that starts from context, not from scratch.”

Speaker cue

Use the phrase “I want to lower the cognitive load on the AE.”

Role play

How I would run the cold-call or discovery portion of the interview.

Open

Earn permission quickly.
Frame the call around a traffic-safety gap, not a pitch deck.

Explore

Find the risky corridor.
Find the current enforcement limitation.
Find the internal blocker.

Advance

Propose a narrow next meeting.
Anchor on proof and pilot logic.
If I get unexpected objections
Acknowledge the concern without arguing.
Clarify whether it is political, operational, or budgetary.
Reframe toward a narrow pilot or data-gathering next step.
If they ask what I would do differently
I would name one question I should have asked earlier.
I would explain how I would tighten the next attempt rather than defending the first one.
I would show coachability without sounding uncertain.
Debrief answer I would use

“I would check whether I found the actual operational bottleneck early enough. If I stayed too broad, I would tighten the next version around one corridor, one stakeholder path, and one proof point.”

What they want to see

Calm opening, clear questions, no panic on objections, and a measured next-step ask.

Do not over-index on

Trying to “win” the role play. The goal is conversational control and diagnosis.

Speaker cue

Say “I am listening for constraints more than buying signals in the first three minutes.”
