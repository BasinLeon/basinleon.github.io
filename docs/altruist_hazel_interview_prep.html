Likely Questions

Voice-practice ready

Tell me about yourself.

I’m a GTM systems builder. I’ve spent the last few years building the infrastructure underneath growth, not just running the motion on top of it. At Fudo I helped build a signal-based operating model that drove 160% pipeline growth and saved about $424K a year through automation. At Sense I helped support systems behind more than $10M in qualified pipeline. More recently I’ve been building Python-, Clay-, and API-driven GTM workflows directly. That’s why Hazel is interesting to me. It’s a systems role.

20 sec: I’ve been building the systems under GTM for a while now. Hazel is compelling because that is literally the job.

Why Altruist and why Hazel?

Two reasons. One, wealth management is a serious category. Trust matters. Precision matters. Two, Hazel still feels early enough that the GTM systems layer actually matters. I’m most useful when a product has traction but the commercial engine still needs to be architected. That’s where I can help most: lead quality, lifecycle logic, rep productivity, instrumentation, and taking manual work out of the system.

20 sec: Hazel is early, real, and trust-heavy. That’s exactly where good GTM systems create leverage.

You haven’t had the exact title GTM Engineer. Why this role now?

That’s fair. I’d answer it directly: the title is newer than the work. The work itself is what I’ve already been doing. I’ve built routing logic, enrichment flows, enablement systems, funnel instrumentation, and AI-assisted GTM workflows in real operating environments. What I like about this role is that the title finally matches the function I’ve been moving toward.

20 sec: I’m newer to the title than the function. The work is familiar.

How technical are you really?

I’m technical enough to build useful systems end to end, especially around automation, enrichment, APIs, CRM extensions, and GTM data flow. I’m strongest in Python-oriented automation, logic design, and connecting tools into a commercial workflow that people actually use. I’m not trying to present myself as a pure product engineer. I’m a GTM operator who can really build.

20 sec: I’m technical where it matters for GTM: APIs, automation, CRM logic, and data flow.

What would you focus on in your first 90 days?

First I’d map the current commercial system end to end: data sources, handoffs, lifecycle stages, enrichment gaps, rep workflows, and where manual work is slowing things down. Then I’d pick one or two high-leverage fixes, probably around signal quality, routing, instrumentation, or rep productivity. By day 90, I’d want productionized improvements that are already reducing friction and giving leadership a cleaner view of the funnel.

20 sec: Map the system, find the friction, fix the highest-leverage loop, and make the funnel easier to see.

How do you work with AEs, marketing, and product?

I usually end up being the translation layer. Sales describes pain in workflow terms. Marketing describes it in funnel terms. Product describes it in systems or event terms. My job is to turn that into shared logic with clear inputs, rules, and outputs. I’ve done that a lot, and honestly it’s one of the parts of the work I enjoy most.

20 sec: I translate between functions and turn vague pain into usable operating logic.

What kind of AI-powered GTM workflows have you actually built?

For me, the right pattern is not just dropping an LLM into a workflow and hoping for magic. I use AI where judgment helps, but I keep the core data quality, routing, and handoff logic deterministic. I’ve used Python, APIs, and tools like Clay to support enrichment, account research, prioritization, and messaging support, but I want those systems tied to clear inputs and measurable outputs. In a regulated category, that matters even more.

20 sec: I use AI where judgment helps, but I keep the core commercial logic clean and deterministic.

How would you support AEs on calls if a technical question comes up?

I’m comfortable stepping in when the question is really about workflow logic, integrations, data movement, or what the system can support operationally. I’m not going to bluff product depth I don’t have, but I can usually clarify what’s possible, what data is needed, and what the downstream workflow would look like. That helps the AE stay credible and keeps the conversation moving.

20 sec: I can support the workflow and data side live, and I’m careful not to fake what I don’t know.

Why do you think you’d do well in a regulated wealth environment?

I’m drawn to businesses where operational precision matters because bad data and sloppy workflows actually have consequences. My Google experience gave me a strong respect for reliability and process discipline, and a lot of my GTM work has required careful logic around routing, attribution, and handoffs. Hazel is interesting because it combines speed with a category where trust still has to be earned.

20 sec: I like high-trust environments because they reward clean systems, not just speed.

If They Push Here

Stay calm
Title gap
You can say: “That’s fair. The title is newer for me than the function, but the actual work is not.”
Technical depth
You can say: “I’m strongest where code meets commercial workflow: APIs, automation, enrichment, CRM logic, and funnel instrumentation.”
Why leave consulting
You can say: “I want to own one system deeply, not just advise around a bunch of systems from the outside.”

What To Emphasize

Repeat these
  • Systems thinker: say you build the system under GTM, not just the campaigns on top of it.
  • Builder: mention Python, Clay, APIs, Salesforce / HubSpot logic, and AI workflows in plain English.
  • Revenue loop owner: connect every technical example to pipeline quality, speed, rep leverage, or cleaner decisions.
  • Regulated comfort: lightly mention Google ops discipline and why clean systems matter in trust-heavy environments.
  • Founder-grade framing: show that you think commercially, but speak like someone who can still open the terminal and wire the system.

Do Not Do This

Avoid
  • Don’t say “I built 690,000 lines of code” unless they ask. It sounds inflated and pulls focus off the real point.
  • Don’t overuse Basin::Nexus language, “signal refinery,” or internal frameworks unless they ask for depth.
  • Don’t sound like a consultant talking about possibilities. Sound like an operator who can own systems.
  • Don’t list every tool you’ve ever touched. Lead with the problem, then the system, then the result.
