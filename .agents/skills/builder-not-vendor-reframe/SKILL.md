# Builder-Not-Vendor Reframe

## The Problem

When you have visible frameworks, a portfolio site, and active consulting clients, interviewers and recruiters will naturally wonder: **are you trying to sell your system, or are you trying to build ours?**

Melissa Little flagged this directly during the Hazel AI screen (Mar 11, 2026): "I just want to be upfront — if I were to introduce someone trying to sell their go-to-market philosophy... that could be problematic for them from a time perspective."

This skill provides tested language and positioning patterns to land as **builder who brings tools** rather than **vendor who brings products**.

---

## Two Failure Modes to Avoid

### 1. Coming Across as a Vendor
**Symptoms:**
- Showing proprietary frameworks unprompted
- Using phrases like "my system," "my product," "my platform"
- Spending more time on your toolkit than on their problem
- Making the conversation about what you've already built rather than what you'd build for them

**What the interviewer hears:** "This person is going to push their existing stack on us instead of building what we actually need."

### 2. Underselling the Consulting Experience
**Symptoms:**
- Apologizing for doing fractional work
- Framing consulting as "not having a real job"
- Hiding the client work instead of positioning it as founding-stage build experience

**What the interviewer hears:** "This person doesn't have real commitment or staying power."

---

## The Correct Frame: Builder Who Brings a Toolkit

### Core Language Pattern

> "I come with a toolkit — scoring models, routing logic, enrichment patterns that I've built and validated across multiple environments. But the goal is always to build with the tools and systems the company already uses, or needs to adopt. The toolkit helps me move faster and avoid common failure modes, not replace what the team needs."

### Tested Variations by Context

**When asked about proprietary frameworks:**
> "These are internal tools I've built to do the job better — they give me a larger working dataset and help me scale faster. But they're not a product I sell. Think of it like a carpenter who brings their own tools to a job site."

**When asked "are you looking for full-time or consulting?":**
> "I want to join one company, build with the team, and grow the system over time. The fractional work was necessary to understand what's on the other side — I've done it for six months, I've learned what I needed to learn, and now I want to build something durable."

**When asked about why you're leaving consulting:**
> "Fractional is useful for range, but you can't build deep systems across three or four companies at once. I do my best work when I'm inside the organization, close to the data, close to the team, and close to the decisions."

**When showing your portfolio during a screen share:**
> Start with: "Let me show you how I think about this problem" — NOT "Let me show you my product."
> End with: "This is the kind of thing I'd build for [Company] based on the specific ICP and sales motion you described."

---

## Pre-Interview Checklist

Before any screen or call, run through these:

- [ ] Am I leading with THEIR problem or MY frameworks?
- [ ] Have I prepared one story about building FROM SCRATCH at a company (not consulting)?
- [ ] Can I describe what I'd build in the first 30 days using THEIR stack?
- [ ] Am I ready to say "I come with tools, but I build with yours" if the vendor question comes up?
- [ ] Have I framed the consulting period as a bridge, not a lifestyle?

---

## Positioning the Fractional Period

The fractional period (Nov 2025 – Present) should always be positioned as:

1. **Bridge work** — "I needed to understand the consulting side to know I want to build inside a company."
2. **Proof of range** — "I shipped systems for a sales training company and a technical sales automation company in parallel. That's range."
3. **Current, not past** — "I'm still building. The SCU guest lecture, the advisory work — it's all active. I'm not between things."

Never say:
- "I'm between jobs"
- "I'm freelancing"
- "I couldn't find the right thing"

Always say:
- "I launched my own practice to build systems across multiple environments simultaneously"
- "I'm doing the work now — I just want to do it inside one company I believe in"

---

## The "Why This Company" Lock

After handling the vendor/builder reframe, always close with a company-specific reason that proves you want to build WITH them, not sell TO them:

**Template:**
> "What makes [Company] interesting to me is [specific company problem or opportunity]. That's the kind of system I want to build and own over time — not advise on from the outside."

**Hazel example:**
> "The organic traction you described — the $100M stock movement with zero marketing — tells me the product has pull. What's missing is the commercial infrastructure to capture and route that demand. That's exactly what I build, and I want to build it here, not advise on it."

## When to Use This Skill

- Before any recruiter screen or hiring manager call
- When preparing Loom walkthroughs of your work
- When writing follow-up emails after interviews
- When updating your resume or portfolio for a specific role
- When someone directly asks "are you trying to sell your framework?"
