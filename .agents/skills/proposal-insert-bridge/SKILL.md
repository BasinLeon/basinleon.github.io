# Proposal Insert Bridge

## Overview

Use this skill when the raw material is messy but the output needs to be short, clear, and easy for a champion to forward internally.

The job is not to write a full proposal. The job is to create the insert that explains why this specialist is in the deal, what they add, and how the buyer can start without replacing existing systems.

## When to Use

- A partner already has a broader proposal and needs "your part" inserted cleanly.
- The current draft sounds like a freelancer bio, service menu, or generic consulting blurb.
- The buyer is warm and already inside a proposal or referral flow.
- The offer should start as a pilot, layer, or add-on rather than a full rebuild.

## When Not to Use

- Full proposal rewrites from scratch.
- Cold outbound where the reader has no context.
- Deep technical scopes that need implementation detail or architecture diagrams.

For those cases, use existing skills such as `revenue-architect-proposal-audit`, `diagnostic-led-outreach`, `executive-brief-writer`, or `copywriting`.

## Inputs

- The current proposal, email thread, transcript, or PDF.
- The internal champion and the economic buyer.
- What the broader proposal already covers.
- The specialist's narrow role in the flow.
- The safest first engagement shape.

## Workflow

1. Lock the role first.
   Default frame: the specialist is the execution layer, activation layer, or pilot layer. Not a generic consultant.
2. Lock the buyer moment.
   The insert is for a warm, in-flow buyer who needs a clean explanation, not a homepage visitor.
3. Default to low-friction adoption.
   Keep the client's current system intact unless the source material proves otherwise.
4. Use a 4-block structure.
   Title, one strategic paragraph, three short bullets, one pilot close.
5. Write for forwarding.
   Use plain language a gatekeeper can send to the real decision-maker without extra explanation.
6. Keep the specialist non-fragile.
   The page should imply a repeatable operating model, not founder dependency.

## Output Rules

- Keep it to roughly half a page or one clean screen.
- Avoid tool names, stack detail, and platform internals unless the buyer already expects them.
- Do not lead with biography, credentials, or long methodology lists.
- Do not ask for deep integration on page one.
- End with a small next step: pilot, comparison, walkthrough, or scoped test.
