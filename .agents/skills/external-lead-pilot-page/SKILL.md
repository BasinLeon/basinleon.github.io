# External Lead Pilot Page

## Overview

This skill creates a page that does two jobs at once:

- gives a partner or seller something concrete to show now,
- creates the skeleton of a real lead-capture asset we can build and reuse.

The default move is a narrow pilot page, not a giant product site. The page should promise one clear outcome, protect the buyer from disruption, and move them to one next step.

## When to Use

- The offer already exists and needs a showable page or section.
- The first sale is a pilot that sits outside the client's current systems.
- You need one reusable asset for both sales conversations and inbound capture.
- Current page ideas are too broad, too abstract, or too dependent on a founder explanation.

## When Not to Use

- Full multi-page site architecture.
- Complex product apps or portals.
- Pages that must explain several unrelated offers at equal weight.

Use `frontend-design` when you are building the actual interface. Use `copywriting` when you only need page copy. Use this skill when the structural page logic is the missing piece.

## Inputs

- Audience and buyer stage.
- Single pilot promise.
- Existing proof points or credibility markers.
- Primary CTA and handoff destination.
- Any constraints from the partner proposal.

## Workflow

1. Keep the promise narrow.
   Default promise: additional qualified leads, cleaner activation, faster handoff, or better routing.
2. Use a 5-section page.
   Hero, no-disruption proof strip, three-step system flow, proof block, short form or booking CTA.
3. Use one primary CTA above the fold.
   Do not split attention across equal-weight choices.
4. Make the page feel safe.
   Name what stays intact: current CRM, current reps, current process, current team.
5. Shorten the ask.
   Collect only the information needed for follow-up. Longer diagnostics belong later in the flow.
6. Prevent dead ends.
   Every section should point to the next step or reinforce why the visitor should continue.

## Output Rules

- One dominant CTA.
- Minimal fields.
- No feature grid with four equally weighted offers.
- No footer that just stops the journey.
- Mobile-friendly section count and copy length.
- If a diagnostic exists, keep it secondary unless the entire page is the diagnostic page.
