# Recruiter Relationship Engine

Use this skill when writing to or preparing for conversations with:

- recruiters
- hiring managers
- headhunters
- talent partners
- talent pools

## Objective
Land as a strong, memorable builder who should stay in motion for the right seat.

The goal is not to "sound interested."
The goal is to:

- create recall
- reduce recruiter uncertainty
- translate Leon's builder/operator background cleanly
- preserve future opportunity even when timing is off

## Core Frame
Leon is not a generic candidate.

He is:

- a builder
- a systems operator
- commercially literate
- someone who can own a hard problem end-to-end

## Three Modes

### 1. Initial Reachout
Use when introducing yourself after applying or after a warm intro.

Structure:
- state the action taken
- identify the overlap
- give one strong positioning line
- make a light ask or simply create recall

### 2. Active Process
Use when already in motion with a recruiter or hiring manager.

Structure:
- confirm the next step
- reinforce fit in one sentence
- remove friction

### 3. Closeout / Future Reopen
Use when a role is filled or timing is off.

Structure:
- acknowledge the outcome
- keep tone calm
- ask to stay on radar only if useful

Do not plead.

## Voice Rules

- warm
- direct
- low-friction
- senior
- never needy

## Recruiter-Specific Principles

### 1. Make their job easier
Recruiters are pattern matchers under time pressure.
Help them answer:

- where does Leon fit?
- why now?
- what kind of seat should he be remembered for?

### 2. Translate the operator profile
If consulting/fractional work is visible, use the builder-not-vendor frame.

Useful line:

*"I come with tools and patterns I've built, but the goal is always to build inside the company's environment, not sell a framework from the outside."*

### 3. Avoid update-chasing
Do not keep asking if there is news.
Only follow up when:

- there is new relevant signal
- they invited follow-up
- enough time has passed to justify a clean nudge

## LinkedIn Reply Pattern

### Strong
*"Thanks, Michelle. I appreciate that. Clearco is exactly the kind of company I'd be excited to build with, so happy to stay on your radar for the right revenue seat."*

### Weak
*"Thanks so much! Please let me know if there are any updates and I would love any chance to interview."*

## Email / DM Templates

### After applying
*"I just submitted for [role]. My background sits at the intersection of operator-led GTM, revenue systems, and founder empathy, so I wanted to put myself on your radar in case the fit is useful."*

### After positive recruiter response
*"Appreciate it. Happy to stay close for the right seat."*

### After rejection
*"Understood. Thanks for the update. If a role opens up that leans more heavily into [specific strength], I'd be glad to stay on your radar."*

## Anti-Patterns

- do not beg for updates
- do not over-explain your whole background
- do not sound like a consultant pitching services
- do not ask for calls before there is a reason
- do not send walls of text on LinkedIn

## What Good Looks Like

- recruiters remember Leon clearly
- future-fit roles reopen naturally
- hiring managers see builder energy, not candidate anxiety
- communication stays short and commercially sharp

## Principle

The best recruiter communication creates optionality without lowering status.
