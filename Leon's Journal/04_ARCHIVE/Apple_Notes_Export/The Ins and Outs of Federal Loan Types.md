<div><b><h2>Pros?and Cons</h2></b></div>
<div><b>Pros</b></div>
<ul>
<li>		If you currently have federal student loans that are with different loan servicers, consolidation can greatly simplify loan repayment by giving you a single loan with just one monthly bill.</li>
<li>		Consolidation can lower your monthly payment by giving you a longer?period of time?(up to 30 years) to repay your loans.?</li>
<li>		If you consolidate loans other than Direct Loans, consolidation may give you access to additional income-driven repayment plan options and?Public Service Loan Forgiveness (PSLF). (Direct Loans are from the William D. Ford Federal?Direct Loan?Program.)?</li>
<li>		YouÕll be able to switch any variable-rate loans you have to a fixed interest rate.</li>
</ul>
<div><b>Cons</b></div>
<ul>
<li>		Because consolidation usually increases the?period of time?you have to repay your loans, you will likely make more payments and pay more in interest than if you didnÕt consolidate.</li>
<li>		When you consolidate your loans, any?outstanding interest?on the loans that you consolidate becomes a part of the original?principal?balance on your consolidation loan, which means that interest may accrue on a higher principal balance than might have been the case if you had not consolidated.</li>
<li>		Consolidation may also cause you to lose certain borrower benefitsÑsuch as interest rate discounts, principal rebates, or some loan cancellation benefitsÑthat are associated with your current loans.</li>
<li>		If youÕre paying your current loans under an income-driven repayment plan, or if youÕve made qualifying payments toward PSLF, consolidating your current loans will cause you to lose credit for any payments made toward?income-driven repayment plan forgiveness?or PSLF.</li>
</ul>
<div><br></div>
<div><br></div>
<div>It's important to know what happens when you don't pay interest as it accrues on Direct Unsubsidized and Direct PLUS loans.</div>
<div><br></div>
<div>The unpaid interest is added to your outstanding principal balance, which makes what you actually owe greater than the loan amount you initially took out. Then interest is charged on that unpaid interest, which is called capitalization. Under most repayment plans, this will increase your monthly payment amount and the total amount you repay over the life of the loan.</div>
<div>When Interest Capitalizes Under the LawÊ</div>
<div>There are certain points specified by law, where interest is capitalized:</div>
<ul>
<li>Following periods of deferment on an unsubsidized loan and/or forbearance on any types of loans</li>
<li>Following the grace period on an unsubsidized loan</li>
<li>If you voluntarily leave the Revised Pay As You Earn, Pay As You Earn (PAYE), or Income-Based Repayment (IBR) plans</li>
<li>If you fail to annually update your income for some of the income-driven plans</li>
<li>If you are repaying your loans under the PAYE or IBR plans and no longer qualify to make payments based on income</li>
</ul>
<div><br></div>
<div><br></div>
<div><b><h2>What Happens With Accrued Interest?</h2></b></div>
<div><b>Show Loan DetailsÊ</b><br></div>
<div>When your repayment period starts, the interest capitalizesÑthat is, the unpaid interest will be added to the loan principal. The sum of the accrued interest plus principal becomes the new principal amount, making your loan more expensive because instead of just owing interest on the principal, you will owe interest on top of the interest!</div>
<div><br></div>
<div>This is where your loan types really matter.</div>
<div><b>Direct Subsidized Loans</b></div>
<div>The federal government has paid your interest, so your repayment will not have any capitalized interest on these loans when you start repayment. However, if you experience any of the triggering events, such as failure to pay or deferring payments, you will be responsible for the interest portion of your loan.</div>
<div><b>Other Loan Types, Including Unsubsidized and Private</b></div>
<div>You are responsible for all interest charged since the funds were disbursed. Any interest that is not yet paid back when your repayment starts will capitalize, thus getting added onto the amount disbursed and increasing your total loan balance.</div>
<div><b><h2>What if Interest Capitalizes on My Loan?</h2></b></div>
<div>When your grace period ends:</div>
<ul>
<li>If you pay off the interest before you enter repayment, your principal will remain the same.</li>
<li>If you do not pay off the interest before you enter repayment, interest will capitalize so your new principal will be the amount of your original principal plus the outstanding interest. Interest will be charged on this new, higher principal.</li>

<div><b><h2>Tips to Meet Your Obligations</h2></b></div>
<div><br></div>
<div>The following tips can help you better meet your student loan repayment obligations:</div>
<div><br></div>
<div><b>Know Your Loan Terms</b></div>
<div>Learn the terms of your loan agreement and understand the effects of paying your loans off early or paying your loans late. Keep your records handy for future reference and to clear up any potential disputes.</div>
<div><br></div>
<div><br></div>
<div><b>Finish Your Program on Time</b></div>
<div>The quicker you finish your program, the sooner you can become employed in your chosen field and be in a better financial position to repay your loans.</div>
<div><br></div>
<div><br></div>
<div><b>Know Where to Go for Help With Your Loans</b></div>
<div>CanÕt afford your monthly payment? Need help comparing your repayment options? Your loan servicer is available to help answer your student loan questions. DonÕt hesitate to contact themÑit's free.</div>
<div><br></div>
<div><br></div>
<div><b>Keep Your Contact Information Up to Date</b></div>
<div>Notify your servicer if your address, name, email address, or phone number?changes, so that you continue to receive your bills and important information about your loans.</div>
<div><br></div>
<div><br></div>
<div><b>Pay on Time</b></div>
<div>It is very important that you make your federal student loan payments on time. If you are having trouble making your monthly payment, you should immediately contact your federal loan servicer for assistance.</div>
<div><br></div>
<div><br></div>
<div>While you have some important obligations as a federal student loan borrower, you also have rights, options, and benefits. As a federal student loan borrower, you can:</div>
<div><br></div>
<div><b>Pay Your Loans Off Early Without Penalty</b></div>
<div>You can pay your loans off in full anytime without fees or penalties. You can also make payments when youÕre not required to and make additional payments toward your principal at any time, which helps you pay on a shorter schedule.</div>
<div><br></div>
<div><br></div>
<div><b>Change Repayment Plans Anytime</b></div>
<div>Although you may select or be assigned the Standard Repayment Plan when you first begin repaying your student?loan, you can change repayment plans at any time for free and without penalty.</div>
<div><br></div>
<div><br></div>
<div><b>Save Money by Enrolling in Automatic Payments</b></div>
<div>If you enroll in automatic payments through your servicer, you have an opportunity to lower your interest rate by .25%.</div>
<div><br></div>
<div><br></div>
<div><b>Flexible Payment Options</b></div>
<div>Although you may select or be assigned the Standard Repayment Plan when you first begin repaying your student?loan, you can change repayment plans at any time for free.?You also have an opportunity to lower your interest rate by .25% by enrolling in auto-debit.</div>
<div><br></div>
<div><br></div>
<div><b>Access the Federal Student Aid (FSA) Ombudsman</b></div>
<div>If you are unable to resolve an issue by working with your loan servicer, you may contact the FSA Ombudsman for assistance. The FSA Ombudsman works with federal student loan borrowers to resolve disputes or issues from an impartial, independent viewpoint.</div>

<div><b><h2>Additional Benefits You Should Know</h2></b></div>
<div><br></div>
<div>WeÕll explain this in more detail a little later in the counseling, but you may also be eligible to:</div>
<div><br></div>
<div><b>Pause Payments</b></div>
<div>If you run into hardship, you can use tools like forbearance and deferment, which we will explain later on, to temporarily get payment relief.</div>
<div><br></div>
<div><br></div>
<div><b>Change Your Payment Due Date</b></div>
<div>If making a payment on another date makes it easier to do so, contact your servicer.</div>
<div><br></div>
<div><br></div>
<div><b>Consolidate Your Loans</b></div>
<div>If you have multiple student loans, you may be able to combine them into one loan with a fixed interest rate based on the average of the interest rates on the loans being consolidated. This could lower your payment.</div>
<div><br></div>
<div><br></div>
<div><b>Apply to Receive Student Loan Forgiveness</b></div>
<div>Programs such as Teacher Loan Forgiveness and Public Service Loan Forgiveness can be a path to savings over the long term if thatÕs your career plan.</div>
<div><br></div>
<div><br></div>
<div><b>Deduct Student Loan Interest on Your Taxes</b></div>
<div>If you meet certain income requirements, you may be eligible to take a?tax deduction for the interest paid on student loans?that you took out for yourself, your spouse, or your dependent. This benefit applies to all loans (not just federal student loans) used to pay for higher education expenses. The maximum deduction is $2,500 a year.</div>
<div><br></div>
<div><br></div>
<div><b><h2>See if you qualify for a lower monthly payment</h2></b></div>
<div><br></div>
<div>It's possible that you could qualify for a lower monthly payment by switching your repayment plan. This can be a better long-term strategy that allows you to stay in repayment status.For example, income-driven repayment plans are typically capped at a certain percentage of your income and offer forgiveness after you've repaid for a certain number of yearsÑusually 20 or 25 years.</div>
<div><b>Pros:</b></div>
<ul>
<li>		Monthly payments should be more manageable compared to other types of repayment plans.</li>
<li>		It can set your monthly payment to $0 if your income is low enough.</li>
<li>		It qualifies for forgiveness through both an income-driven repayment plan and Public Service Loan Forgiveness.</li>
</ul>
<div><b>Cons:</b></div>
<ul>
<li>		You may pay more interest in the long term.</li>
<li>		Any amount that is forgiven under an income-driven repayment plan (not Public Service Loan Forgiveness) is treated as income that you may need to pay tax on.</li>
</ul>
