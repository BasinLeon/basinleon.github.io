<div>Privileged Access Management (PAM) encompasses a range of terms, concepts, and technologies designed to secure and control access to critical systems and sensitive data by users or accounts with elevated privileges. Some of the most common terms include:</div>
<div><br></div>
<div>1. Privileged Account:</div>
<div>An account with elevated permissions, often granting access to critical systems, sensitive data, or administrative functions that standard user accounts do not have. Examples include domain administrators, root accounts, or service accounts.</div>
<div><br></div>
<div>2. Privileged Credentials:</div>
<div>Authentication information (such as passwords, keys, tokens, or certificates) that allows access to privileged accounts. The management of these credentials is a central focus of PAM solutions.</div>
<div><br></div>
<div>3. Credential Vault:</div>
<div>A secure repository where privileged credentials are stored, managed, and accessed. The vault protects against unauthorized access and ensures that credentials are rotated, checked out, and monitored.</div>
<div><br></div>
<div>4. Least Privilege Principle:</div>
<div>A security concept where users or processes are given only the minimum level of access required to perform their tasks. Applying least privilege reduces the risk of misuse or compromise of privileged accounts.</div>
<div><br></div>
<div>5. Session Management:</div>
<div>A feature of PAM solutions that enables secure, audited connections to sensitive systems. Session management often includes session recording, monitoring, and the ability to terminate sessions if suspicious activity is detected.</div>
<div><br></div>
<div>6. Password Rotation:</div>
<div>The regular updating of privileged account passwords to reduce the risk of unauthorized access due to credential theft or sharing. Automated rotation is often a core function of PAM tools.</div>
<div><br></div>
<div>7. Just-in-Time Access (JIT):</div>
<div>A PAM practice that provides privileged access only for the time needed to complete a specific task. This reduces the attack surface and limits the exposure of privileged credentials.</div>
<div><br></div>
<div>8. Privileged Task Automation:</div>
<div>Using automated workflows to handle routine administrative tasks, thereby reducing the need for human interaction with privileged accounts and decreasing the likelihood of human error.</div>
<div><br></div>
<div>9. Superuser Account:</div>
<div>A highly privileged account, such as root on Unix/Linux systems or the Administrator account in Windows environments. These accounts have unrestricted access to system resources.</div>
<div><br></div>
<div>10. Access Policies:</div>
<div>Rules that define how, when, and by whom privileged accounts can be accessed. Policies often include multifactor authentication (MFA), approval workflows, and geographic or time-based restrictions.</div>
<div><br></div>
<div>11. Audit and Compliance Reporting:</div>
<div>A PAM function that tracks and documents privileged account usage, providing logs and reports to meet regulatory compliance standards and security best practices.</div>
<div><br></div>
<div>12. Endpoint Privilege Management (EPM):</div>
<div>A component of PAM that focuses on managing privileges at the endpoint level, ensuring that users do not operate with full admin rights by default.</div>
<div><br></div>
<div>13. Risk-Based Authentication:</div>
<div>An approach that adjusts authentication requirements based on the perceived risk of the request. For example, accessing a privileged account from an unknown location might trigger additional verification steps.</div>
<div><br></div>
<div>14. Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA):</div>
<div>A security measure requiring more than one form of verification (e.g., password + hardware token) before granting access to a privileged account.</div>
<div><br></div>
<div>15. Separation of Duties (SoD):</div>
<div>A security principle where no single individual has control over all aspects of a critical process. In PAM, this might involve separating account creation, credential management, and approval roles to prevent misuse of privileged accounts.</div>
<div><br></div>
<div>These terms provide a foundation for understanding PAM and how it is implemented to secure critical systems and protect against cyber threats.</div>

<div><br></div>

<div>The shift to remote work during the COVID-19 pandemic accelerated the adoption of PAM tools. <div><br></div>
<div>3. Adoption in Critical Infrastructure and Industrial IoT (IIoT):</div>
<div>Industries like energy, manufacturing, and utilities are increasingly deploying PAM to protect industrial control systems (ICS) and IIoT devices.
