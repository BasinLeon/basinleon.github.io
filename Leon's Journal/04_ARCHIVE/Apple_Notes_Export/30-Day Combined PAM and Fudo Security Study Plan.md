<div><b>Core Concepts &amp Deep Explanations (PAM Essentials)</b></div>
<div><b><br></b></div>
<div><b>1. Privileged Account</b></div>
<div><b><br></b></div>
<div><b>A privileged account has elevated permissions that allow users to access critical systems and make changes. Examples include:</b></div>
<div><b>	ź	System administrators</b></div>
<div><b>	ź	Database administrators</b></div>
<div><b>	ź	Service accounts for automation</b></div>
<div><b><br></b></div>
<div><b>Why itšs Important: Privileged accounts are high-value targets for attackers because they provide access to sensitive data and system controls.</b><br></div>
<div><br></div>
<div><b>2. Principle of Least Privilege (PoLP)</b></div>
<div><b><br></b></div>
<div><b>The least privilege principle ensures that users have only the access necessary to perform their jobčnothing more.</b></div>
<div><b><br></b></div>
<div><b>Example:</b></div>
<div><b>	ź	An HR employee should have access to payroll data but not server configurations.</b></div>
<div><b>	ź	A database admin should not have access to marketing data.</b></div>
<div><b><br></b></div>
<div><b>Benefits: Reduces the attack surface and limits the damage of insider threats.</b><br></div>
<div><br></div>
<div><b>3. Just-In-Time (JIT) Access</b></div>
<div><b><br></b></div>
<div><b>JIT access grants temporary privileged access when needed and removes it once the task is complete.</b></div>
<div><b><br></b></div>
<div><b>Why it Matters:</b></div>
<div><b>	ź	Reduces exposure to high-risk privileges.</b></div>
<div><b>	ź	Prevents permanent elevated access, minimizing insider threats.</b><br></div>
<div><br></div>
<div><b>4. Password Vault</b></div>
<div><b><br></b></div>
<div><b>A password vault securely stores and manages credentials for privileged accounts. It often supports automatic password rotation and access auditing.</b></div>
<div><b><br></b></div>
<div><b>How it Helps:</b></div>
<div><b>	ź	Prevents password reuse.</b></div>
<div><b>	ź	Reduces the risk of credential theft.</b><br></div>
<div><br></div>
<div><b>5. Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA)</b></div>
<div><b><br></b></div>
<div><b>MFA adds an extra layer of security by requiring multiple forms of verification (e.g., a password and a push notification on your phone).</b></div>
<div><b><br></b></div>
<div><b>Why itšs Critical for PAM:</b></div>
<div><b>	ź	Reduces the chance of unauthorized access, even if the password is compromised.</b><br></div>
<div><br></div>
<div><b>6. Zero Trust Security</b></div>
<div><b><br></b></div>
<div><b>Zero Trust means ęnever trust, always verify.ė Every request for access is treated as if it comes from an untrusted source.</b></div>
<div><b><br></b></div>
<div><b>Key Principles:</b></div>
<div><b>	ź	Least Privilege Access</b></div>
<div><b>	ź	Continuous Verification</b></div>
<div><b>	ź	Micro-Segmentation (isolating different parts of the network)</b></div>
<div><b><br></b></div>
<div><b>PAMšs Role in Zero Trust: Enforces least privilege and verifies every access request for critical systems.</b><br></div>

<div><b>General Questions</b></div>
<div>	1.	<b>What is Privileged Access Management (PAM), and why is it important?</b></div>
<div><b>Sample Answer: PAM protects critical systems by managing and securing privileged accounts, reducing the risk of breaches and insider threats. It enforces least privilege access and provides detailed audit logs for compliance.</b></div>
<div>	2.	<b>How does PAM support a Zero Trust security strategy?</b></div>
<div><b>Sample Answer: PAM enforces least privilege and provides continuous verification, ensuring that access to critical resources is granted only when necessary and always verified.</b></div>
<div>	3.	<b>Describe Just-In-Time (JIT) access and its benefits.</b></div>
<div><b>Sample Answer: JIT access minimizes security risks by granting temporary privileges only when needed. This reduces the attack surface and limits exposure to high-risk accounts.</b><br></div>
