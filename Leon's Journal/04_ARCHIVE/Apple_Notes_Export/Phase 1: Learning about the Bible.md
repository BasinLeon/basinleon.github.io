<div>Here's a three-part Bible Study method that's been working well for me:</div>
<div><br></div>
<div>Step 1: Observation (85% of your time)</div>
<div><br></div>
<div>A good detective starts by collecting data without making too many assumptions about what happened, and thatÕs how you can read the Bible too.</div>
<div><br></div>
<div>Start by attempting to understand the text. Ground yourself in its context. Ask questions like: Where does the story take place? Who is speaking? How does this story relate to the rest of Scripture?</div>
<div><br></div>
<div>Be as objective as you can. Most of the work of Bible Study is simply to understand whatÕs happening, what's being said, and why each word is worth the ink on the page.</div>
<div><br></div>
<div>Step 2: Interpretation (10% of your time)</div>
<div><br></div>
<div>This is the stage in a detectiveÕs search where they start connecting the dots to see what kind of image is emerging. They know itÕs best to wait until they have a keen understanding of the events that took place, and it can be wise to do the same with your Bible study.</div>
<div><br></div>
<div>Look at the big picture. Step away from the details and try to figure out what the author is trying to say, based on the observations youÕve made.</div>
<div><br></div>
<div>Step 3: Application (5% of your time)</div>
<div><br></div>
<div>This is the practical part of Bible Study when you can start thinking about what the text means for your life.</div>
<div><br></div>
<div>Most people see this final step as the essence of Bible Study, even though it may be better to spend the least of your time on it.</div>
<div><br></div>
<div>The problem with leaping to this final step is that youÕre bound to miss the point by interpreting scripture through the lens of your life instead of interpreting your life through the lens of scripture.</div>
<div><br></div>
