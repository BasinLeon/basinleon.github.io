04 Likely Questions + Answers Tap to Expand ▾
Q Walk me through your outbound process. ▶
Framework

I don't start with a sequence — I start with a signal architecture. Before any email or call goes out, I want to know: what's the ICP, what signals indicate a good-fit account right now, and what's the enrichment layer that makes my outreach relevant instead of generic.

At Fudo, that meant building a Python-powered enrichment pipeline that pulled technographic, firmographic, and intent signals before a rep ever touched a lead. At Obvio, the equivalent would be mapping municipalities by traffic fatality rate, recent council votes on public safety budgets, and incumbent tech relationships — so every touch is timed and contextual.

From there: personalized first touch tied to a specific signal, multi-channel follow-up with decreasing cadence, and a clear handoff protocol to the AE when interest is qualified. The system logs everything into CRM so the AE walks in with context, not cold data.

Q How do you handle a cold territory? ▶
Strategy

A cold territory is an infrastructure problem, not a hustle problem. If no one's heard of you, flooding them with email volume doesn't fix it — it burns the list.

First move: identify the highest-signal accounts. For Obvio, that's cities with recent pedestrian fatalities, active public safety bond measures, or visible media coverage of traffic issues. Those are accounts where the problem is already salient — I'm not creating urgency, I'm arriving at the right moment.

Second: find warm paths. City councils, public safety associations, regional conferences — these are the channels that bypass cold outreach entirely. At Fudo, I used RSA prep to generate warm conversations before the conference started. Same principle: warm the territory before you blast it.

Third: build the feedback loop early. First 30 days of outreach tells you what messaging lands and what doesn't. I instrument that at the sequence level so I'm iterating fast, not repeating bad patterns for a quarter.

Q What does good pipeline hygiene look like to you? ▶
Ops

Pipeline hygiene means the data in the CRM reflects reality — not what we wish were true. Stage definitions are clear and enforced. Contact coverage maps the full buying committee, not just the first person who responded. Activity is logged at the deal level, not just the contact level.

For a company like Obvio selling into government, I'd add: procurement stage tracking. Government contracts have distinct milestones — RFP, budget approval, council vote. If those aren't mapped in the CRM, forecast accuracy is a guess. I'd want to build that stage structure early, before deals pile up without it.

The goal is that Mike can walk into a pipeline review and trust the number — not spend the meeting doing archaeology on old deals.

Q Why BDR vs. a more senior role? ▶
Framing

Direct answer: because Obvio is at the stage where the BDR function is the company's revenue foundation. There's no playbook yet. The person who takes this role isn't just generating meetings — they're building the repeatable pipeline motion that the whole go-to-market will run on.

I've been at the senior operator level. I know what a broken top-of-funnel does to an AE's quarter, to forecasting, to morale. Coming in at BDR here means I'm building the infrastructure I know needs to exist — not inheriting someone else's half-built version of it.

Also: I want to earn the trust. At 8 employees, I'd rather show what I can build in the first 90 days and have that conversation based on results — not a title I walked in with.
