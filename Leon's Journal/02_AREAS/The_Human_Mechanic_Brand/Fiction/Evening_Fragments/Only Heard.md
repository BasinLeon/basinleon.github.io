# Only Heard

The cursor stopped blinking whenever I wrote a sentence that wasn't true.

At first, I assumed it was a rendering bug—some minor latency spike in the text editor. I killed the terminal process, cleared the local cache, opened a fresh buffer. Same static pulse. 

*I'm fine,* I typed. 

The cursor froze. A dead black line at the end of the *e*. No pulse. No blink. Like the editor itself had decided not to participate in the performance.

I deleted the words and tried again. 

*I'm just tired.*

Nothing. It sat on the screen like a cold diagnostic. 

Under the desk, the dog slept warm against my ankle with the full weight of a small old promise. Outside, Sunnyvale had gone soft: low headlights sweeping past, garage doors sliding shut, a neighbor dragging bins to the curb. Ordinary systems running on ordinary time. Obedient ones.

I sat there in the blue glow for a long time, staring at the dead terminal. 

Then I typed:

*I don't know who I am when nobody needs anything from me.*

The cursor blinked. 

Once. 

Then again, steady and rhythmic as a second heart. Under the desk, the dog didn't stir, but the room seemed to widen. It felt like the sentence hadn't been accepted, exactly. Just heard.

***

By 1:00 AM, the house began answering only the things I didn't say.

I went to the kitchen for water, standing barefoot on the cold tile. The refrigerator hummed with its quiet, domestic confidence, keeping its own temperature in the dark. 

I stood there and thought, *Tomorrow will be easier.*

A floorboard in the hallway bent under no one's weight, a sharp wooden pop that broke the quiet. 

I held the glass with both hands and looked down the hall. The kids slept behind half-closed doors. Their rooms were full of soft proof: shoes kicked sideways, library books left open on the rug, small shirts folded badly in laundry baskets. A whole world constructed on the assumption that the man in the kitchen knew what he was doing. 

I almost thought: *They don't know how scared I am.*

The house went dead silent. Not peaceful. Waiting. 

Finally, I whispered it into the dark. "I don't know if I'm actually becoming better, or if I'm just getting better at hiding the parts that aren't."

The copper pipes knocked once inside the wall, a deep, resonant tick. 

Then the refrigerator resumed its hum, and the air moved again. 

***

At 2:17, the dog woke up.

Not all the way. Just enough to lift his graying chin from the rug and look at me from the doorway with the exhausted patience of a creature who had never required a brand or a slide deck to recognize me. 

I was back at the desk. The document was still open, the line waiting. 

I typed: 

*I'm doing all of this for them.*

The dog stood. Slowly, his old joints clicking in the silence. 

I deleted the line. 

I typed: 

*I want them to be proud of me.*

The dog stayed standing, his dark eyes steady, refusing to lie down. 

The room had gone cold. The cracked window let in the smell of damp jasmine and asphalt. Somewhere miles away, a car moved too fast down El Camino Real, its engine whining before disappearing into the larger dark. 

I looked at the dog. I deleted the sentence. 

Then I typed: 

*I want them to feel free around me, even when I am disappointed in myself.*

The dog lowered his front legs, then his hips, letting out a long, shuddering sigh as he settled back into the rug. The warm ballast of his body returned to sleep. 

On the screen, the cursor blinked beside the punctuation. 

Not approval. Company.

***

The morning was too bright. 

By 7:00 AM, Sunnyvale looked exactly like it had the day before: the light flat and clean across the laminate, the coffee maker clicking through its pre-programmed cycle. The night felt like a localized glitch, an atmospheric pressure that had evaporated with the dew. 

My daughter sat at the kitchen island, her knees pulled up inside her oversized sweater, her thumb scrolling through something bright and fast on her phone. 

"Are you okay?" she asked, not looking up from her screen. 

She was ten. She didn't mean *are you tired.* She meant: *Is the system still holding? Are you the anchor, or are we drifting?*

I opened my mouth to say the easy thing. The sentence I had pre-compiled for three different clients and two recruiters this week. The one that keeps the room calm.

*Yeah, sweetie. Just a long night.*

The pendant light above the island flickered. Just once. A sudden, sharp drop in the room’s current that left us in shadow for a fraction of a second. 

My daughter stopped scrolling. She looked up at the bulb, then at me.

I looked at my coffee cup. The dark surface was perfectly still. 

"I'm trying to be," I said.

The pendant bulb stayed bright, casting a clean, unwavering circle of warm light across the counter. 

She looked at me for a second longer—really looked, past the operator, past the father who optimized every pipeline, searching for the man underneath. Then she let out a quiet breath and went back to her phone. 

"Okay," she murmured. 

And for the first time in years, the kitchen felt large.
