Source: Mike Venable, April 2, 2026. These are not hypotheticals. They are direct from the Colorado field.

"Cameras are controversial here after the Flock/ICE story." Obvio has no federal data sharing. Locally controlled, solar-powered. We're not Flock. We're what cities wanted before the cameras got political. Pure safety data, no surveillance angle.
"Our city council would never approve this." That's usually because nobody from the community has told them they want it. We've seen councils flip when cycling and pedestrian groups show up. Want to start there before we go to council?
"We don't have budget for something like this." No upfront cost. We install, we maintain. City costs are offset by citation revenue over the 5-year contract. Most customers are revenue-neutral by month four.
"We tried a vendor before and it didn't go well." Verra Mobility has had issues in several California cities. We're a different stack. Solar, AI-scored, no legacy hardware. Can I show you what Fort Collins looked like switching?
"This isn't one of my projects this year." Fair. Can I put devices at the 2–3 spots you know are dangerous and give you real data? No commitment. If you want to bring it to council later, you'll have something solid instead of anecdotes.
"We already have a traffic enforcement program." What's it covering? Most programs are 3–5 cameras on top corridors with nothing near schools. We can complement what's in place or scale coverage without adding headcount.
"People will push back on AI enforcement." Lead with the data, not the technology. Residents complain about traffic safety more than almost anything. The ask isn't "approve AI." It's "let us show you where people are running stop signs near your schools."
